Situation Briefing
Why did Venezuelan democracy collapse?
Structural, institutional, and economic roots of the crisis

Venezuela did not lose its democracy in a single coup. It was lost progressively: first through institutional co-optation, then through selective repression, and finally through hegemonic state control. Restoring it requires understanding each layer of that collapse.

Structural Causes of the Collapse
DimensionWhat happenedInstitutional consequence
Executive Power Rule-by-decree via permanent emergency powers; concentration of authority in the presidency; elimination of institutional checks and balances. The National Assembly lost its real legislative function. The Supreme Court became an instrument of the executive.
Judiciary The Supreme Court (TSJ) was politically packed under Chávez (2004). Independent judges were removed or self-censored. No independent arbiter remains. TSJ rulings served to legitimize the concentration of power.
Electoral System The CNE was controlled by ruling-party loyalists. Electoral rules were changed arbitrarily, candidates were banned, and state media was weaponized. Elections without minimum guarantees. A permanent legitimacy crisis around results.
Economy & Oil PDVSA was used as a political cash machine. When oil prices collapsed (2014–16), the model failed. Hyperinflation and collapse of basic services followed. 7+ million Venezuelans emigrated. The state's human capital was effectively destroyed.
Military & Control Militarization of the state: armed forces control companies, ports, and food distribution. Corrupt officers protect the regime in exchange for impunity. The military is an autonomous actor with its own interests. Any transition requires its neutrality or support.
Repression SEBIN, DGCIM, and armed colectivos used against opponents. Documented political prisoners, forced exiles, and torture. Fragmented opposition. Intimidated civil society. A pervasive culture of fear.
Foreign Actors Cuba provided intelligence and political guidance. Russia and China provided loans and diplomatic cover. The U.S. imposed broad economic sanctions. Venezuela as a geopolitical arena. The regime's survival depends on external allies.
Key question: Which institutions are recoverable and which need to be rebuilt from scratch? The Supreme Court (TSJ), the CNE, the Attorney General's office, and the armed forces are the four critical axes of any negotiation.
Actor Mapping
Who is at the table?
Click each card to see the positions, interests, and red lines of each actor. In the simulation, each group represents one of these parties.
Government / Ruling Party
Chavista Establishment (PSUV)
Representatives of the ruling PSUV party, cabinet ministers, and state apparatus loyalists. They hold state power but face internal pressure, international sanctions, and a deepening legitimacy crisis.
On elections
Will only accept elections if there are guarantees of no criminal prosecution after the transition. Demand "political amnesty" as a precondition for any deal.
Red line: Criminal immunity
On the CNE
Accept CNE renewal only if the opposition cedes proportional representation and does not pre-emptively challenge electoral results.
On the military
Armed forces must remain in their current institutional role. Purges of the military high command are unacceptable.
Red line: Integrity of the military high command
Transitional government
Will only accept a national unity government with equal PSUV-opposition representation. Reject any "technocratic" government that excludes the PSUV from political power.
Democratic Opposition
Unified Democratic Platform (PUD)
A heterogeneous coalition of opposition parties — from social democrats to liberals and center-right. Fractured internally over tactics (negotiation vs. maximum pressure) but united in rejecting the regime.
On elections
Demand presidential elections within a maximum of 18 months, with robust international observation (EU, UN, Carter Center), an audited voter roll, and no candidate bans.
Red line: Banned candidates must be free to run
On the CNE
The CNE must be dissolved and reconstituted with independent commissioners proposed by civil society and ratified by a legitimate National Assembly.
On transitional justice
Accept transitional justice mechanisms (truth, reparations, non-recurrence guarantees) but reject blanket amnesty for crimes against humanity.
Transitional government
Propose a 7-member State Council: 3 opposition, 2 civil society, 1 ruling party, 1 business sector. Maximum mandate of 24 months.
Armed Forces
FANB — Reformist Factions
Mid- and high-ranking officers who have signaled openness to a negotiated transition, on condition that the armed forces retain their constitutional role, their institutional benefits, and are not subjected to mass purges.
Core condition
Active neutrality: the armed forces will not back the regime if it is guaranteed there will be no special military tribunals or extraditions for drug trafficking charges during the transition.
Red line: No extradition of officers to the United States
On security
The military will maintain public order during the transition. Armed colectivos must be demobilized gradually — not abruptly.
On politics
Support creation of a civilian transitional government. Will not participate directly in politics and will not accept being assigned political responsibility for the crisis.
Civil Society
Civil Society Organizations (CSOs)
Human rights NGOs, professional associations, labor unions, churches, and community networks. They represent Venezuelans not affiliated with any party — and are often the most directly affected by the crisis.
Priority 1: Human rights
Demand the immediate release of all political prisoners as a precondition for any formal negotiation. Document 272+ political prisoners (Foro Penal, 2024).
Red line: No release of prisoners = no negotiating table
Priority 2: Participation
Demand a guaranteed seat in any transitional government. Will not accept being treated as secondary actors in a bilateral deal between the regime and the opposition.
On justice
Support a "Truth Commission" model with victim participation. Reject amnesties without acknowledgment of responsibility.
International Community
Foreign Actors
The U.S., EU, Latin American countries (Mexico, Brazil, Colombia), and bodies such as the OAS and UN. Divided between those who favor maximum pressure and those who prefer facilitated negotiation.
USA / Europe
Offer to lift sanctions in exchange for verifiable elections. Condition economic assistance on compliance with a democratic roadmap. Demand freedom for all political prisoners.
Mexico / Brazil / Colombia
Prefer a negotiated solution without external imposition. Offer to serve as process guarantors. Oppose sanctions as the primary tool of pressure.
Russia / China / Cuba
Support the "sovereignty" of the Maduro government. Would only accept a transition if PSUV retains political influence. Block resolutions in the UN Security Council.
Role in the transition
UN and Carter Center electoral supervision; funding for institutional reconstruction; ongoing compliance verification.
Venezuelan Diaspora
7+ Million Venezuelans Abroad
The largest diaspora in Latin America. Significant concentrations in Colombia, Peru, Chile, Spain, and the U.S. They hold formal political rights but face major practical obstacles to voting. Highly mobilized politically.
Central demand
Guaranteed overseas voting, access to consulates, updated voter registries, and logistical support. The regime historically suppressed these mechanisms.
Red line: Right to vote from abroad
On return
Demand security guarantees for voluntary return. A reintegration program with degree recognition as part of the transition agreement.

Negotiation Simulation
The negotiating table
This is the space for active negotiation. Each group must identify its possible compromises and its absolute red lines before seeking consensus with other parties.
Forum instruction: A transition can only happen if ALL actors accept the agreed framework. Without the ruling party, there is no peaceful transition. Without the opposition, there is no legitimacy. Without the military, there is no order. Without civil society, there is no representation.
Zones of possible agreement
Possible compromises (concessions)
On the timeline: All actors show flexibility on timing. A window of 18–24 months for presidential elections could be acceptable to all parties if backed by real, verifiable guarantees.
On the CNE: A mixed CNE with non-partisan technical commissioners selected by consensus and internationally audited could bridge the gap between "full dissolution" and "continuity."
On sanctions: A progressive sanctions-lifting mechanism tied to verifiable democratic milestones could align the ruling party's incentives with the requirements of a genuine agreement.
On government composition: A 9-member State Council — 2 PSUV, 3 opposition, 2 civil society, 1 economic sector, 1 diaspora representative — with a rotating presidency every 6 months.
On the military: The armed forces guarantee neutrality and security of the process. In exchange: no purges or extraditions during the transition. Human rights cases are referred to the Truth Commission, not to foreign courts.
Absolute red lines (non-negotiable)
Ruling party: No PSUV leader can be referred to international criminal courts as a condition of the agreement. Without this guarantee, the process cannot advance.
Opposition: Candidates banned by the regime — including María Corina Machado and others — must be permitted to stand for election. No elections without free candidacies.
Military: No extradition of Venezuelan military personnel to the United States or other countries on drug trafficking charges for the duration of the transition agreement.
Civil society: The release of political prisoners is not a bargaining chip — it is a precondition for formal talks to begin. No prisoners released, no negotiating table.
International community (US/EU): Will not recognize any transitional government that does not include a verifiable electoral timeline with independent international observation.

Outcome A — Framework Transition Agreement
All parties sign a minimum viable agreement
Conditions for this to be possible: The ruling party receives non-prosecution guarantees during the transition. The opposition secures an electoral timeline with real oversight. The military gets institutional protection. Civil society has guaranteed representation. International actors certify the process.

5-phase implementation plan:

  1. Month 0–1 — Founding Agreement: Signature of the "Caracas Pact" by all actors. Release of political prisoners as a good-faith gesture. International announcement of the process begins.
  2. Month 1–3 — Installation of the State Council: Constitution of the 9-member transitional government. Appointment of the Transitional CNE with UN validation. Beginning of voter roll audit.
  3. Month 3–9 — Stabilization: Progressive sanction lifting tied to verifiable milestones. Truth Commission begins its work. Gradual demobilization of colectivos. Partial reconstitution of the Supreme Court.
  4. Month 9–18 — Electoral preparation: Candidate registration opens (no political bans enforced). Consulates open for diaspora voter registration. Electoral campaign with equitable media access for all parties.
  5. Month 18–24 — Elections and transfer of power: Presidential, parliamentary, and regional elections held in a single cycle. Full international observation. Peaceful transfer of power to the elected government.
Statement to the Venezuelan people: "The parties gathered in this forum have reached a framework agreement for Venezuela's democratic transition. This agreement does not fully satisfy any single party — that is the sign that it is fair. Together, we commit to a transparent, verified, and peaceful path toward free elections."
⚠️
Outcome B — Partial Agreement with a Roadmap
Minimum consensus on some issues; harder issues deferred for later rounds
Scenario: The parties cannot resolve the hardest issues (amnesty, CNE reform, candidate bans) but do agree on a procedural framework and a roadmap with clear timelines and designated arbiters.
  1. Immediate agreement: Release of 50% of political prisoners within 30 days. Installation of a permanent dialogue mechanism with international guarantors. Cessation of repressive operations against opposition figures and civil society.
  2. Negotiating agenda: CNE reform, candidate bans, and amnesty remain on the agenda with a 6-month deadline to resolve. Process mediated by Mexico and Brazil.
  3. Caretaker government: A bipartisan technical caretaker government — not a full transition government — is installed to manage the economy and public services while negotiations continue.
  4. Breakpoint: If no agreement is reached on blocked issues within 6 months, Outcome C is automatically triggered.
Statement: "We have reached significant partial agreements and commit to continuing the dialogue. Venezuela deserves a complete solution. We will keep working."
💔
Outcome C — Breakdown of Negotiations
Parties declare the collapse of the process and inform the country
Scenario: The ruling party walks out after failing to secure impunity guarantees. Or the opposition withdraws as candidate bans remain in place. Or the military refuses to guarantee neutrality. The process collapses entirely.
  1. Statement of failure: Each party publicly explains why agreement was not possible and who bears responsibility for the collapse. Transparency is essential for maintaining legitimacy before the Venezuelan people.
  2. Immediate consequences: Deepening of international sanctions. Possible U.S. and EU recognition of an alternative government. New waves of social mobilization inside and outside Venezuela.
  3. Alternative scenarios: (a) Maximum pressure strategy until regime collapse; (b) Internal fracture of chavismo that opens a new negotiating window; (c) Indefinite prolongation of the status quo with continued humanitarian collapse.
  4. Civil society's role: CSOs take on the critical role of keeping the democratic demand alive, documenting abuses, and preserving the cohesion of the human rights movement through the impasse.
Statement to the Venezuelan people: "We regret to inform you that the dialogue process did not reach an agreement. [Responsible party] refused to accept the minimum conditions for a democratic transition. We will continue to seek paths to guarantee the rights of the Venezuelan people. Democracy is not dead — it is being resisted."
Reflection question: What mistakes did the negotiators make? What single concession might have changed the outcome? What is the difference between a legitimate red line and a maximalist position that destroys the entire process?
Final Reflection Questions
1. Is democracy possible without justice? Or is impunity the unavoidable price of a peaceful transition?
2. What role should the international community play without crossing into a form of neo-colonial interference in Venezuelan sovereignty?
3. Can the armed forces simultaneously serve as guarantors of the transition and remain suspects in human rights crimes? How is that contradiction resolved?
4. Who has the right to define what a "successful transition" looks like — the negotiators, the Venezuelan people, or the international community?
